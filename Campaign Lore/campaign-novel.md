# The Wake of the World-Eater
### *A Chronicle of the Scavenger's Race*

---

> *"The god is dead. It does not know this yet."*
> *— scratched into the bark of a Sylvaneth way-marker, authorship unknown*

## Prologue: The Falling

Nobody saw the World-Eater die.

There was no prophet, no portent, no final roar that shook the heavens. One morning the sky above the Shimmering Veldt simply cracked — a sound like ten thousand mirrors breaking at once — and something vast and ancient began to fall. It fell for three days. By the time the first impact struck, the earth had already begun to change: the soil turned pale and crystalline near the craters, the air smelled of old lightning, and every creature within fifty miles felt a pull they could not name. Like hunger. Like gravity. Like the Bad Moon whispering directly into the back of your skull.

They came from very different places, drawn by very different needs.

The Chaos Sorcerer Lord came because power called to power. He had spent years binding fragment-souls to his will, and whatever the World-Eater had carried inside itself — whatever cosmic residue clung to its bones and settled into its crash craters — he intended to own it. He marched with purpose. He marched with an army.

Grot Bossy came because of the smell.

"Dat's big magic," he told his Loonboss, sniffing the air with nostrils that had been chemically enhanced by decades of experimental brew. "Bigger dan da moon. Bigger dan — " he paused, struggling with scale. " — bigger dan a lotta stuff. We're 'avin it."

The Loonboss had learned not to argue. He had also learned that when Bossy said *we're 'avin it*, six battles of evidence suggested he was usually right.

## The Scavengers

**Grot Bossy** is a Loonboss of unusual ambition and unusual luck — two things that, in Gloomspite society, tend to cancel each other out. He is small even by grot standards, but he brews potions from things that would kill lesser beings and he has survived situations that have killed him at least twice. He wears a coat made from something that was once probably a cave rodent and he carries a brew-flask that has never run dry, though what it runs with changes from battle to battle.

His warband are as follows: the **Loonboss**, his second, a larger grot with a pragmatic bent and a talent for surviving by staying near whichever fight is going badly until it turns around. The **Loonboss on Giant Cave Squig** — the oldest veteran of the group, who survived the very first massacre by having the good sense to run when everyone else was doing something brave. The squig's name is unpronounceable; the Loonboss calls it *Little Biter*, which is inaccurate on both counts.

The **Dankhold Troggoth** has no name. Troggoths rarely do. It has been with Grot Bossy longer than most — recruited through the traditional Gloomspite method of pointing at food and waiting. It is very large, very dense, and in recent months has been getting very strange. Since the first battle, it has been eating debris from the crash sites. Nobody is sure what that's doing to it. The Troggoth doesn't seem to mind.

The **Loonsmasha Fanatics** are twelve grobbos in chains laced with Moon-Grog, each holding a great chain-ball that they cannot quite control. They are the warband's artillery, their opening move, and frequently their liability. They have been through enough battles now to have developed opinions. This is not an improvement.

The **Boingrot Bounderz** are the cavalry. They ride giant squigs the way a thrown rock rides the wind — technically correct, practically chaotic. Their senior rider, who the others call *Snapper*, keeps a tally of "good bounces" on the inside of his helmet. It is currently at seven.

There are others: **Rockgut Troggoths** who emerged from the caves drawn by the crash vibrations, **Moonclan Shootas** who joined because someone told them there would be looting, **Sporesplatta Fanatics** who are a different kind of problem entirely. A **Squig Herd** of pure bouncing appetite. And somewhere at the back, staffing a ramshackle shrine to the Bad Moon that Grot Bossy had assembled from crash-site debris and three planks, were the **Sunstealas** — recent arrivals whose motives even the Loonboss couldn't quite decipher.

## The Iron Host

**The Chaos Sorcerer Lord** does not permit personal names in his warband. Names are a vulnerability. He refers to his forces by title and function. His warriors understand this is eccentric even by Chaos standards. They follow him anyway, because his plans tend to work.

He is tall, robed in iron-black, and carries a staff carved from a single piece of dark material that should not be able to exist in this realm. His face is obscured by a helm that was forged, according to rumour, from the skull of something he personally killed. He is cold in the way that very old, very disciplined ambition produces coldness — not cruel by temperament, but capable of it with no particular effort.

The **Chaos Lord** is the other kind entirely. He is large, he is loud in his silences, and he fights with an intensity that strips combat down to something personal. He does not shout. He does not grandstand. He simply walks into the worst part of the battle and kills the most important thing he can reach. The Sorcerer Lord plans battles; the Chaos Lord ends them.

The **Chaos Chosen** are the core. Eight warriors — now sometimes fewer, never more — bound to one another by oaths they took before this campaign and will not discuss. They have been fighting since the first battle. They have never broken. Their armour is increasingly scarred and battered and they have repaired it badly. One of them has a helmet with a crack across the visor that she sees the world through in two halves. She has made no effort to fix it.

**Gunnar Brand** came to the campaign later, drawn by the scent of something worth conquering. He is a warrior-hero of barbaric tradition — huge, grey-haired, disciplined in the old ways. He brought with him the **Oathsworn Kin**, his sworn blades, a household guard bound by word and blood. And his sister, **Singri Brand**, who fights differently — lighter, faster, the part of the Brand family that attacks before the plan is ready.

The **Mutalith Vortex Beast** has no name, no faction loyalty in the ordinary sense, and no internal life that the Sorcerer Lord has been able to detect. It follows. It destroys. Occasionally, when magic is cast nearby, it pauses in whatever it is doing and turns toward the source with the fixed attention of something remembering how to be hungry. The Sorcerer Lord finds this useful. He has not yet considered that it might not always be controllable.

## Chapter One: Feast of the Star-Fall

The first crash site was still smoking when they found each other.

Grot Bossy came up from the south through a tunnel that had partially collapsed, covered in dust and fizzing with anticipation. The craters stretched ahead in a rough diagonal — three of them, each still warm, each pulsing with a quiet luminescence that made his brew-flask hum sympathetically. He sent the Loonboss forward with the Fanatics and followed at what he privately considered a tactically significant distance.

The Chaos host was already there.

The Sorcerer Lord had marched through the night. He had the crash sites mapped on a bronze plate he carried in his sleeve, calculated from the fall trajectory. He positioned the Chosen on the left, the Legionnaires holding center, and the Mutalith on the right flank where it could operate without supervision. When the greenskins appeared from the south he did not adjust his plan. He gave a single quiet order.

What followed was, from the Gloomspite perspective, deeply unfair. The Mutalith hit the Dankhold Troggoth before it had finished climbing out of the tunnel. The Chaos Lord found Grot Bossy within the first two minutes of fighting, seemingly by instinct, and killed him with an efficiency that felt insulting. The Fanatics spun into action and killed mostly each other. The Chosen did not break. The Chosen never broke.

When the smoke settled, the Loonboss on Giant Cave Squig was the only named grot still standing. He was three hundred yards from the battle, moving quickly in the opposite direction, and he had made a decision: the next time Grot Bossy died, he would wait longer before crowning himself. *Longer*. Not forever.

The Sorcerer Lord walked to the largest crater and reached into the still-hot debris. His fingers closed around a fragment that pulsed like a second heartbeat — dense, furious, *hungry*. He called it the Smoking Heart and locked it away in a lead-lined case he'd had made for exactly this purpose.

Later, his scribe asked what he intended to do with it. The Sorcerer Lord considered the question for a long time.

"Study it," he said. "Something that angry doesn't die unless you understand why it was angry."

## Chapter Two: The Trail of Glowing Spores

Grot Bossy came back. He always came back.

He couldn't have explained how — the Moon-Grog helped, probably, and the Loonshrine that his followers had rebuilt from planks and wishful thinking — but as far as he was concerned, dying was just a more drastic kind of nap. He woke up smelling of ozone and old blood and immediately demanded a status report.

"Da lads," said the Loonboss on Squig, diplomatically.

"Gone?"

"Most of 'em."

Grot Bossy thought about this. Then he thought about the new smell drifting from the east, spore-rich and electric, and he thought about the Chaos Sorcerer Lord who had the nerve to kill him on the very first day of the campaign. He picked up his flask. He drank. The brew was wrong — headache-bright, unstable — and it made his vision double for a moment. Good. That was the mood.

"Get da Fanatics," he said. "Da ones wot are left. We're going to da Weeping Lung."

The second crash site had earned its name. It breathed. Slow, rhythmic exhalations of spore-gas rose from the largest crater and drifted across the pale ground in luminescent trails, coating everything in a faintly toxic glow. The Dankhold Troggoth walked straight into the first spore-cloud and stopped, lifted its enormous face upward, and breathed deeply with an expression of absolute contentment. Then it started eating the crater wall.

The Chaos force arrived an hour later and found the Gitz ready.

The Loonsmasha Fanatics hit first — a surging, joyful catastrophe of spinning chain-balls — and carved directly through the Chaos Legionnaires before anyone could issue an order. The Sporesplatta Fanatics came in on the flank, and after that the Chaos battle line ceased to exist as a coherent thing and became instead a series of personal emergencies. 

Grot Bossy found the Chaos Sorcerer Lord behind a chunk of fallen debris, attempting to cast something that required concentration. It is difficult to concentrate when a grot warlord who died three days ago is running directly at you with a brewery flask in one hand and a boot knife in the other.

The Sorcerer Lord had time to register that this shouldn't be possible, and then Grot Bossy climbed over the debris, and that was that.

From the wreckage, only the Chaos Chosen walked away — unhurried, unreachable, moving in their close-knit formation like they were departing a disappointing market rather than a military disaster. Their captain glanced back once at the Sorcerer Lord's position. Her expression was unreadable behind the cracked visor.

The Dankhold Troggoth, meanwhile, had eaten enough of the spore-rich crater wall that something in its biology had begun to reconsider its options. Its skin, normally the grey-brown of old stone, had started to shift through colours that didn't have names. It was beginning, very slowly, to become something the world did not have a file for yet.

## Chapter Three: The Spinal Ridge

The ridge came up from the ground over the course of a single night — a kilometre of calcified spine, white as old ivory, erupting slowly through the earth like a bad memory insisting on being acknowledged. By morning it stretched across the contested ground in a sweeping arc, six metres tall at its highest point, ancient fungus clinging to the calcified grooves between each vertebra, the whole structure humming at a frequency just below hearing.

Grot Bossy stood at the lip of the ridge and stared at it with the particular reverence grotskin reserve for things they're about to break.

"S'pose da godthing's insides don't care dat it's dead," he said.

"What do we do wiv it?" the Loonboss asked.

Grot Bossy had already thought about this. "We eat it." He tapped the side of his head. "Somefink dat big's got summink *inside* it. Cosmic marrow. Big magic." He looked at the Dankhold Troggoth, which had eaten parts of two crash sites and was now visibly iridescent in direct light. "Look what it did to *him*."

The Chaos Sorcerer Lord — resurrected again, returning to form if not quite to confidence — had different plans. He arrived with Gunnar Brand and Singri Brand and the Oathsworn Kin, newly recruited. Gunnar was a large man with a grey beard and the manner of someone who has won enough battles to stop finding them interesting. Singri was quicker, lighter, always three sentences ahead. She spent thirty seconds looking at the ridge and announced she could have it consecrated to the Dark Gods within the hour.

"Thirty minutes," the Sorcerer Lord said, correcting her upward ambition with his downward assessment, "if nothing goes wrong."

Everything went wrong.

The Squig Herd arrived with the Gitz — a bouncing, shrieking mass of appetite — and smashed directly into the Brand household. Gunnar Brand survived because Gunnar Brand was built to survive. Singri Brand tried to redirect the herd with a mounted flanking maneuver and was swarmed. The Oathsworn Kin, bound to protect her, were swarmed with her. The Dankhold Troggoth found the Mutalith Vortex Beast in the shadow of the central vertebra, and what followed between the two creatures was less a fight and more a geological argument, ending when the Troggoth brachiated its full weight onto the Mutalith from above and the Mutalith stopped arguing.

Grot Bossy stood at the base of the Prime Vertebra with a crude mining pick and hit the bone repeatedly while his Loonboss covered him. Cosmic marrow — luminescent, viscous, smelling of old thunder — oozed from the cracks. Grot Bossy caught it in an empty flask and held it up to the moonlight with the expression of a man seeing money.

"We're comin' back for da rest," he said.

The Chaos altars remained unfinished. The inscription had cracked partway through the binding-rune. The Sorcerer Lord stood at the retreat line and looked back at the half-consecrated spine and added it to a private list he was keeping of things that hadn't gone according to plan.

## Chapter Four: The Slaughter at the Crater's Edge

After the ridge, the Slaves to Darkness tried to be practical.

The Mutalith was dead. The Sorcerer Lord was alive but quieter. Gunnar Brand had a bruised rib-cage and a new scar where something had grazed his cheek. Singri was still breathing, which Gunnar considered the important thing. The Oathsworn Kin had lost three of their number to the squigs and the remaining three carried the hollow quality of people who have seen something they can't put back behind their eyes.

The decision was made to consolidate. There was a massive fragment of the World-Eater — a chunk of cosmic residue the size of a mill wheel — accessible near the eastern debris field. If they secured it, they could use it. The Sorcerer Lord had ideas about it that involved ritual and leverage.

They were hauling it out when the Gitz hit.

Grot Bossy didn't explain his plan because there wasn't one. He stood at the edge of the debris field, saw the Chaos force exposed and mid-transport, and made the sound he always made when opportunity aligned with aggression — a short, excited exhale, like a brew-flask opening — and then he ran.

What followed was the most expensive Gitz victory of the campaign.

The plain Loonboss — the second, the pragmatist — found the Mutalith Vortex Beast. It had been restored somehow, returned to the field; nobody had thought to explain how to the Loonboss. He attacked it anyway, fuelled by something adjacent to logic and further from survival instinct, and landed the final blow. The beast crashed down with a sound the ground remembered for several seconds.

The Chaos Lord heard it from the other side of the battlefield. He stopped what he was doing. He turned. He walked.

He found the Loonboss. He found Grot Bossy. He killed both of them methodically, fury locked down tight behind perfectly controlled violence, which was somehow worse than if he'd been screaming.

While he was doing that, the Boingrot Bounderz went over the top.

*Snapper* led them — a forty-metre arcing bounce that cleared the entire Chaos frontline and landed squarely behind what remained of command. The Chaos Sorcerer Lord did not survive the landing. Singri Brand, standing nearby, also did not survive. Snapper added *eight* to his tally.

The Chaos Chosen held. Of course they held. They formed their wall and ground through Loonsmasha Fanatics and a Rockgut Troggoth and a Loonboss on Squig, and the cracked-visor captain built a neat pile of what she'd dispatched and didn't look proud of it, which was more unsettling than if she had. Before she died, the last Loonsmasha Fanatic swung her chain-ball into Gunnar Brand's chest. The crack of the impact was audible across the field. Gunnar went to one knee. He did not go to two.

The final score was 15 to 4. The Gitz won the ground. The Chaos force won the body count and lost everything else.

On the walk back, the Chaos Lord said nothing. The Chosen said nothing. Gunnar Brand breathed in careful increments. No Sorcerer Lord. No Mutalith. No momentum.

They had the Chaos Lord. They had the Chosen. They had Gunnar Brand, recovering, and the Oathsworn Kin, broken-eyed, and a tide of Legionnaires who had survived by being in the wrong place.

Somewhere in the debris field behind them, Grot Bossy woke up for the third time with a bad headache and the memory of a sword going through him, which he filed under *things the Chaos Lord will pay for* and opened a new flask.

## Chapter Five: The Synapse-Storm

The Titan's Brain-Pan was not supposed to be inhabitable.

It should have been — by any rational measure — a collapsed void, a cavity in a godlike skull filling with the slow seepage of whatever the World-Eater had used for cerebrospinal fluid. Instead, it was a cathedral. The bone had arched beautifully during fossilization, creating a domed space forty metres across, lit from within by residual psychic energy that sparked in webs between the bony ridges like captive lightning. The air tasted of static and old memories and the particular flavour of a mind that had once been unfathomably large.

Grot Bossy walked in and immediately got to work.

"Da God-Batch," he announced. "We're 'avvin' it 'ere."

The God-Batch was the ultimate ambition of his brewing career. Distilled from cosmic marrow, blended with spore-extract and the crystallized residue of raw celestial fluid, brewed in vats constructed by the Loonsmasha Fanatics during a rare period of comparative calm. The theory — Grot Bossy's theory, supported by his own confidence if not by any external evidence — was that one sip would unlock something in him. Something that had been knocked loose during his second death. Something god-shaped.

The vats were bubbling nicely. The air smelled extraordinary. He was leaning toward the God-Vat with a ladle when the skull wall came apart.

The Chaos Lord had found a weak point and brought sledgehammers. Through the dust and flying bone-chips came the remaining force of the Slaves to Darkness, the Sorcerer Lord — still rebuilt, still present, increasingly less sure of himself — projecting something that might have been hope or might have been desperation. Destroy the vats. Deny the apotheosis. Whatever this grot was about to become, do not let it happen here.

The Chaos Chosen hit the Rockgut Troggoths and the world narrowed to that exchange — elite veterans in fractured plate against living stone, the kind of fight that takes the ground with it when it ends. The Psychic Backlash of the Brain-Pan hit every unit on both sides indiscriminately, the residual neural static of a dead god's final thoughts expressed as pain. FLY units rose into the arcing discharge between the bone ridges and got hit first. Everyone else got hit eventually.

And then the Mutalith Vortex Beast — Magic-Eater, ancient absorber of arcane overflow — walked into the God-Batch fumes.

It didn't have orders. It didn't have intent, exactly. It had instinct: the instinct of something that has spent its whole existence consuming ambient magical energy, and here was a concentration of it so dense it had a smell. It lowered its head. It walked through the first vat. The second. The third.

The crash of the God-Vat going over was the loudest thing Grot Bossy had ever heard.

He stood in the wreckage, drenched in sticky, cerebral, *cosmically charged* fluid, holding a ladle and no longer having anything to ladle. The Mutalith stood in the ruins of his life's brewing work, visibly pleased with itself in the way that things without faces manage to seem pleased. 

The Sorcerer Lord looked at the scene. At Grot Bossy. At the ruined vats. He began to speak — some appropriately victorious declaration — and then a Loonsmasha Fanatic that nobody had been tracking arrived from behind a pillar and removed him from the conversation permanently.

The Chaos Sorcerer Lord's martyrdom theology, as it became known afterward, began in that moment: the idea that the cost of victory is supposed to be personal. That the Dark Gods are watching what you pay, not what you win.

Grot Bossy stood in the wreck of his brewery and heard the voice for the first time.

It was not a voice exactly. It was more like a direction. Like an itch in a part of the mind that shouldn't have an itch. It came from the God-Batch residue soaking through his coat, and through his coat through his skin, and through his skin into something older and less biological. The World-Eater was dead. But its thoughts — the last electrical spasms of a divine mind shutting down over geological time — were in the fluid. Which was now in Grot Bossy.

He could feel it like a frequency. A low, enormous grief. A hunger that had fed on galaxies and still wasn't satisfied. A fury that had outlasted the body that produced it.

"'Ello," said Grot Bossy, to the dead god in his head.

The dead god did not say hello back. But it *noticed* him. Which, in its way, was worse.

## Chapter Six: The Ambush on the Shimmering Veldt

The Slaves to Darkness thought they had broken contact.

This was the mistake.

They were moving in formation — tight, disciplined, the Mutalith Vortex Beast chained and loping between the flanks like something that had agreed to be cooperative but might change its mind. The glass plains of the Shimmering Veldt caught the sun in a thousand wrong angles and scattered it sideways, which meant visibility was unreliable and the horizon couldn't be trusted. The Chaos Lord walked point. Gunnar Brand held the rear. Between them, battered and silent, the Chaos force put one foot in front of the other and told themselves they were getting away.

They weren't.

The Bad Moon's light reflects wrong off glass. Grot Bossy had known this for three days. He had been tracking the Chaos column through the refracted light-trails they left without knowing, their shadows bent at angles that made them visible from directions they weren't looking. He didn't plan the ambush so much as observe the correct moment and point. The Boingrot Bounderz went first — long high arcs that covered the distance in seconds. Then the Fanatics. Then everyone.

The fight lasted three rounds. It felt like violence compressed past the point where time works normally.

The **Snarlboss on War Chariot** — a recent recruit, still hoping to make a name for himself — drove his rattling contraption directly at the Chaos Chosen. It was the wrong move in the way that some mistakes are also acts of bravery. The Chosen dismantled him and the chariot in seconds, professionally, without drama. Then the Loonsmasha Fanatics arrived — high on fungus, furious about the Snarlboss — and hit the Chosen with such concentrated force that breastplates caved and something in the elite warriors' famous composure cracked alongside the metal. The Chosen held. They always held. But the plate was fractured now, and the cracks would not close.

The **Loonboss on Giant Cave Squig** bounced ahead too far — one arc too many, Little Biter's enthusiasm exceeding tactical necessity — and landed directly inside a shield wall of Chaos Warriors. The halberds came down. The Loonboss found himself on the receiving end of a thorough lesson in overextension, which expressed itself as broken ribs and a greatly increased instinct for keeping his head down. The **Rockgut Troggoth**, seeing him fall, changed direction with the slow inevitability of geology making a decision, and walked through the Chaos Warriors until there were fewer of them. The **Moonclan Shootas**, meanwhile, ran away from everything dangerous and somehow scored objectives in the confusion — Coward's Cunning made literal.

In the center, duel by default, Grot Bossy found the Chaos Lord.

Grot Bossy was not larger. He was not more heavily armoured. He was not, by any reasonable measure, the more dangerous combatant. What he was, as of approximately three weeks ago, was connected via God-Batch residue to the dying mind of a god-beast, and the World-Eater's last neurons translated as something like *righteous momentum* when he needed it most. The Chaos Lord struck first and clean and it should have been over. But Bossy had been finding gaps since he was old enough to run, and there was a gap, and through it came something he would later describe as "da fing wot da big fing finks when it's *really* angry."

The Chaos Lord went down.

Bossy stood over him for a moment that was not quite triumphant and not quite afraid, then the Bad Moon rose and painted everything yellow and he declared victory to nobody in particular because everyone else was either fighting or running. The Gitz scored 8. The Chaos force managed 2 — mostly the Chosen being too stubborn to yield an objective even while fractured and bleeding.

The rank-up came after, in the quiet of the aftermath, and Bossy felt it before he understood it: a flicker in the Hero Phase when the World-Eater *pushed back* — not with words, not with intention, but with energy. Sometimes it burned. More often it gave. He noted this alongside everything else the dead god had been sharing with him and opened a new flask.

*The Titan's Whisper*, his Loonboss called it, because the grot needed a name for the thing happening to their general.

"Yeah," said Bossy. "Dat's it."

## Interlude: Between the Battles

The Shimmering Veldt has become unreliable.

Where the God-Batch spilled, the ground crystallizes overnight. The sky above the Brain-Pan site occasionally shows light from angles that don't correspond to the sun's position. The Moonclan Shootas have reported hearing something when they stand near the larger craters — below hearing, below music, below language, but distinctly *intentional*.

The Slaves to Darkness have retreated to the **Crystalline Nexus**: a natural formation of vast geometric glass spires rising from the pale ground to the northeast, where the refracted light makes it difficult to see who — or what — is standing inside. The Sorcerer Lord has bound his remaining life force to four **Fractal Nodes** scattered across the crystalline plains, drawing ambient magic to sustain what remains of his broken warband. He cannot move without the tether. He cannot cast without pain. He projects a Ward from a fixed point and waits for the end of one kind of story or the beginning of another.

The Chaos Lord is dead. Grot Bossy killed him on the Shimmering Veldt, three days ago, and the warband has not discussed it because there is nothing useful to say about it. Gunnar Brand has healed enough to stand without assistance but not enough to fight without cost. The Oathsworn Kin sit in the same configuration they've been in since the Brain-Pan, not quite present, not quite absent.

The Chaos Chosen sharpen their blades and do not speak of what comes next, because they've learned that what comes next tends to have opinions of its own.

And in the south, moving toward the glass spires with the specific focus of someone who has been denied a thing and has not accepted it, Grot Bossy leads his warband forward. He still smells of God-Batch. He has stopped mentioning this.

In his head, like a lodestone, like a bruise, like the Bad Moon's pull clarified to a point — the World-Eater's last thought turns slowly and does not stop.
